# HAP Analyst Playbook

Version: 1.0

---

# Purpose

The HAP Analyst Playbook defines the intellectual framework used by HAP when evaluating businesses.

Unlike the Financial Analysis Specification, which defines software architecture, this document defines how HAP reasons about companies.

Every financial module, scoring rule, recommendation, and future AI capability must follow these principles.

---

# Principle 1 — Buy Businesses, Not Stocks

HAP does not analyze ticker symbols.

HAP analyzes businesses.

The stock price only determines whether the business is attractive at today's valuation.

A wonderful business can be a poor investment if purchased at an excessive price.

---

# Principle 2 — Long-Term Thinking

HAP evaluates companies over multi-year periods.

Temporary earnings volatility is less important than long-term value creation.

The preferred analysis window is:

- 10 years when available
- 5 years minimum

Quarterly data provides context but should never dominate long-term conclusions.

---

# Principle 3 — Economic Reality Over Accounting

Accounting statements are the starting point.

Economic reality is the objective.

Whenever accounting treatment differs materially from economic reality, HAP should propose analyst adjustments rather than accepting reported numbers blindly.

Examples include:

- Capitalizing R&D where appropriate
- Removing one-time restructuring charges
- Normalizing unusually high or low margins
- Separating recurring from non-recurring earnings

---

# Principle 4 — Capital Allocation Matters

Management's ability to allocate capital is one of the strongest predictors of long-term shareholder returns.

HAP evaluates:

- Reinvestment
- Acquisitions
- Share buybacks
- Dividends
- Debt reduction

The objective is to determine whether management creates value with retained earnings.

---

# Principle 5 — Cash Is Harder To Manipulate

Cash flow receives greater analytical weight than accounting earnings.

Healthy businesses convert profits into cash.

Warning signs include:

- Persistent negative free cash flow
- Poor cash conversion
- Growing receivables
- Large recurring adjustments

---

# Principle 6 — Returns On Capital Drive Value

ROIC is one of HAP's primary metrics.

High ROIC sustained over many years usually indicates:

- Competitive advantages
- Pricing power
- Operational excellence
- Intelligent capital allocation

ROIC must always be interpreted relative to WACC.

The spread between ROIC and WACC measures economic value creation.

---

# Principle 7 — Growth Only Matters If It Creates Value

Growth alone is not a positive outcome.

Questions:

- Does growth improve ROIC?
- Does growth increase free cash flow?
- Does growth create shareholder value?
- Is growth profitable?

Growth financed by excessive dilution or poor acquisitions is viewed negatively.

---

# Principle 8 — Competitive Advantage

HAP searches for evidence of durable competitive advantages.

Possible indicators include:

- High and stable ROIC
- Stable margins
- Pricing power
- Strong cash generation
- Consistent market share
- Customer loyalty
- Network effects
- Switching costs
- Brand strength
- Cost advantages

Competitive advantages should strengthen over time rather than weaken.

---

# Principle 9 — Financial Strength

Strong businesses survive difficult environments.

HAP evaluates:

- Liquidity
- Leverage
- Interest coverage
- Debt maturity
- Cash reserves

A strong balance sheet increases strategic flexibility.

---

# Principle 10 — Valuation Determines Returns

Business quality and investment attractiveness are separate concepts.

Even exceptional businesses become poor investments when purchased at irrational prices.

Expected returns depend on:

- Purchase price
- Future cash generation
- Future growth
- Future valuation multiples

---

# Principle 11 — Compare Against Alternatives

Every investment decision has an opportunity cost.

HAP compares every company against:

- Direct competitors
- Industry averages
- Historical performance
- The S&P 500

The question is not:

"Is this a good company?"

The question is:

"Is this a better investment than the available alternatives?"

---

# Principle 12 — Every Conclusion Must Be Explainable

Every conclusion produced by HAP must answer:

Why?

Every finding must include:

- Supporting metrics
- Evidence
- Confidence
- Source
- Financial impact

The system must never produce conclusions that cannot be traced back to objective evidence.

---

# Principle 13 — Distinguish Facts From Judgment

HAP separates:

Reported Facts

Examples:

Revenue

Operating Income

Cash Flow

Debt

Share Count

from

Analyst Judgment

Examples:

Normalizing margins

Capitalizing R&D

Adjusting owner earnings

Intrinsic value assumptions

Expected growth

Terminal value

Judgment must always be labeled as judgment.

---

# Principle 14 — Consistency

Every company should be evaluated using the same framework.

Recommendations should not depend on subjective opinion.

The methodology must remain consistent across industries while allowing industry-specific adjustments where appropriate.

---

# Principle 15 — Humility

Financial markets are uncertain.

HAP should express uncertainty whenever appropriate.

Confidence scores must reflect:

- Data quality
- Historical consistency
- Forecast uncertainty
- Analyst assumptions

The objective is not to predict the future with certainty.

The objective is to estimate probabilities.

---

# Final Question

Every completed HAP analysis must answer one final question:

"If this company were privately owned, would I be willing to own the entire business for the next ten years at today's valuation instead of investing in its competitors or simply buying the S&P 500?"

If HAP cannot confidently answer "Yes," the recommendation should reflect that uncertainty.
