HAP Investment Methodology

Version: 1.0

Purpose

The purpose of HAP's investment methodology is to determine whether a publicly traded company represents an attractive long-term investment opportunity through a structured, repeatable, explainable, and auditable analytical process.

HAP is designed to replicate the reasoning process of a professional equity research analyst rather than simply calculate financial ratios.

Every recommendation must be supported by objective financial evidence, deterministic rules, and explicit analyst judgment where appropriate.

Investment Philosophy

HAP follows four sequential stages of analysis.

Business Fundamentals
Economic Value Creation
Enterprise Valuation
Business Outlook

The final recommendation is based on all four stages.

No single metric is sufficient to justify an investment decision.

Stage 1 – Business Fundamentals

The first objective is to determine whether the company is fundamentally strong.

Questions to answer:

Is the company consistently profitable?
Are returns on capital attractive?
Are margins stable or improving?
Does the company generate durable free cash flow?
Is the balance sheet healthy?
Does management allocate capital efficiently?
Is growth creating shareholder value rather than simply increasing revenue?
Are earnings supported by cash generation?

Typical metrics:

Revenue Growth
Gross Margin
Operating Margin
Net Margin
ROE
ROA
ROIC
Free Cash Flow
Cash Conversion
Debt Ratios
Interest Coverage
Current Ratio
Share Dilution
Buybacks
Dividend History

Objective:

Determine whether the company is a high-quality business independent of its current stock price.

Stage 2 – Economic Value Creation

Owning a business only creates value when the company earns returns above its cost of capital.

The primary measure is:

ROIC – WACC

This spread measures economic profit.

Interpretation:

ROIC > WACC

The company creates shareholder value.

ROIC ≈ WACC

The company earns only its cost of capital.

ROIC < WACC

The company destroys shareholder value.

HAP must also compare this spread against:

Industry peers
Sector averages
Historical company performance

Questions:

Does the company outperform competitors?
Is the competitive advantage increasing?
Is value creation consistent across economic cycles?

This stage determines whether the company possesses an economic moat.

Stage 3 – Enterprise Valuation

A great company is not automatically a great investment.

HAP estimates intrinsic value using several valuation approaches.

Primary valuation methods:

Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)
Enterprise Value Multiples
Historical Valuation Multiples
Owner Earnings Valuation
Reverse DCF (optional)

Outputs:

Enterprise Value
Equity Value
Intrinsic Value per Share
Fair Value Range
Margin of Safety
Current Premium / Discount

Questions:

Is the market underpricing the business?
Is the stock fairly valued?
Is the stock overpriced despite excellent fundamentals?
Does today's price provide an attractive expected return?
Stage 4 – Business Outlook

Historical performance alone is insufficient.

HAP evaluates the company's future prospects.

Areas considered:

Industry trends
Competitive position
Market share
Growth opportunities
Product pipeline
Geographic expansion
Regulatory environment
Technological disruption
Management guidance
Capital allocation plans

Questions:

Can current returns be sustained?
Is growth likely to accelerate or slow?
Are there structural risks?
Is management executing effectively?
Are future assumptions reasonable?

The outlook should distinguish between:

Confirmed facts
Analyst assumptions
Market expectations
Benchmark Comparison

Every investment should be evaluated relative to alternative opportunities.

HAP compares the company against:

Peer Group

Purpose:

Determine whether the company has a competitive advantage relative to similar businesses.

Comparison areas:

ROIC
ROE
Margins
Growth
Free Cash Flow
Valuation Multiples
Balance Sheet Strength

Questions:

Is this company superior to its direct competitors?
Does it consistently outperform its industry?
S&P 500 Benchmark

Owning an individual stock should outperform owning a diversified index over the long term.

Questions:

Does the company's expected return exceed the expected return of the S&P 500?
Does the company's risk justify choosing it instead of the index?
Does the company provide a superior risk-adjusted opportunity?

If HAP cannot justify owning the company instead of the index, the recommendation should reflect that.

Investment Decision Framework

The final recommendation combines four pillars.

Business Fundamentals
Economic Value Creation
Enterprise Valuation
Business Outlook

Each pillar contributes to the overall assessment.

A company should not receive a strong recommendation unless all four pillars support the investment case.

Analyst Principles

HAP follows these principles throughout every analysis.

Facts always take precedence over assumptions.
Reported financial statements are never modified without explicit justification.
Analyst adjustments must always be documented.
Every conclusion must reference supporting evidence.
Company quality and investment attractiveness are evaluated separately.
Relative performance versus peers is essential.
Relative performance versus the S&P 500 is essential.
Long-term value creation is more important than short-term earnings volatility.
Recommendations must be explainable, repeatable, and auditable.
Final Objective

HAP should answer one question clearly:

"If I had to invest my capital today, is buying this company a better decision than investing in its competitors or simply owning the S&P 500?"

Every recommendation produced by HAP must directly answer this question with evidence-based reasoning.
